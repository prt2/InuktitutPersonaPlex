TOPIC: Culture and Traditions in Inuktitut-Speaking Communities
SOURCES: Government of Nunavut (Inuit Societal Values); Nunavut Impact Review Board; Qaujigiartiit Health Research Centre; Nunatsiavut Government; Indigenous Peoples Atlas of Canada; Wikipedia

INUIT QAUJIMAJATUQANGIT — EIGHT PRINCIPLES
The Government of Nunavut and its institutions are formally guided by eight Inuit Qaujimajatuqangit (IQ) principles, also referred to as Inuit Societal Values. Originally six were identified by Elders; the Nunavut government added two more.

1. Inuuqatigiitsiarniq (ᐃᓅᖃᑎᒌᑦᓯᐊᕐᓂᖅ) — respecting others, relationships, and caring for people.
2. Tunnganarniq (ᑐᙵᓇᕐᓂᖅ) — fostering good spirit by being open, welcoming, and inclusive.
3. Pijitsirniq (ᐱᔨᑦᓯᕐᓂᖅ) — serving and providing for family and community.
4. Aajiiqatigiinniq (ᐋᔩᖃᑎᒌᓐᓂᖅ) — decision-making through discussion and consensus.
5. Pilimmaksarniq / Pijariuqsarniq (ᐱᓕᒻᒪᒃᓴᕐᓂᖅ) — development of skills through observation, mentoring, practice, and effort.
6. Piliriqatigiinniq / Ikajuqtigiinniq (ᐃᑲᔪᖅᑎᒌᓐᓂᖅ) — working together for a common cause.
7. Qanuqtuurniq (ᖃᓄᖅᑑᕐᓂᖅ) — being innovative and resourceful.
8. Avatittinnik Kamatsiarniq (ᐊᕙᑎᑦᑎᓐᓂᒃ ᑲᒪᑦᓯᐊᕐᓂᖅ) — respect and care for the land, animals, and environment.

"Qaujimajatuqangit" comes from the Inuktitut verb root "qaujima-" ("to know"), literally translating to "that which has long been known by Inuit." The phrase is frequently abbreviated to "IQ."

INUIT QUALITY CONCEPTS
Additional concepts that appear in Inuit cultural discourse:
- Inunnguiniq — "making a human being"; a holistic approach to raising children that encompasses physical, emotional, spiritual, and intellectual development. Central to Inuit parenting and early childhood practice.
- Ilaginniq — family relations; extended kinship as the basic unit of Inuit society.
- Aqausiq (plural: aqausiit) — a personal song or lullaby, often composed for a specific loved one.
- Sanaugaq — a carved object, artwork; by extension, craftsmanship.
- Piliriqatigiingniq — working together, as a core social value (overlaps with IQ principle 6).

THE QULLIQ — CULTURAL SYMBOL
The qulliq (ᖁᓪᓕᖅ) is a crescent-shaped soapstone oil lamp that, for thousands of years, was the most important object in an Inuit home. It burned rendered seal, whale, or caribou fat with an Arctic cotton grass wick. Functions:
- Provided light through the long polar winter.
- Warmed the iglu or qarmaq.
- Dried clothing and boots.
- Served as a cooking fire.

Tending the qulliq was traditionally a woman's responsibility and required skill — keeping the wick trimmed so the flame burned evenly without smoking. Today, the qulliq is lit at government openings, graduations, and ceremonial events. It is a powerful symbol of Inuit continuity, resilience, and the central role of women in maintaining life.

THROAT SINGING — KATAJJAQ
Katajjaq (also spelled katajjait or qatajjaq) is traditionally performed by two women facing each other closely. Each produces alternating sounds through voiced and unvoiced breathing, rhythmically imitating sounds of animals, wind, water, or daily life. Traditionally it was a game — the performance ended when one singer laughed, ran out of breath, or stumbled. Throat singing was suppressed by missionaries for over a century in many regions. Today it is undergoing strong revitalization, and contemporary artists like Tanya Tagaq have developed solo forms and brought katajjaq to international audiences.

DRUM DANCING
Inuit drum dancing uses a qilaut — a large, flat, single-sided frame drum held by a handle. Unlike many drumming traditions, the qilaut is struck on the rim, not the drumhead. The drum is often paired with ayaya songs, which are personal or communal songs, sometimes passed down for generations and sometimes composed for specific events.

In Nunatsiavut, traditional Inuit drumming was almost lost due to Moravian missionary suppression but has been revived, partly through artists like Deantha Edmunds and community programs.

KAKINIIT AND TUNNIIT — TRADITIONAL TATTOOS
Kakiniit (plural; singular kakiniq) refers to traditional Inuit women's tattoos, typically applied to the face, hands, arms, and thighs. Tunniit refers specifically to facial tattoos. Each pattern carried meaning tied to life milestones, family, and spiritual protection. Beginning in the early 1900s, Christian missionaries pressured Inuit to abandon the practice; by mid-century kakiniit had almost entirely disappeared.

The Inuit Tattoo Revitalization Project, led by Angela Hovak Johnston beginning around 2014, works to teach contemporary Inuit women the traditional skin-stitching and hand-poke methods and revive kakiniit as a living practice. Johnston's book "Reawakening Our Ancestors' Lines: Revitalizing Inuit Traditional Tattooing" (2017) documents the revival.

MORAVIAN MUSIC TRADITION (NUNATSIAVUT)
Moravian missionaries established their first Labrador mission in 1771 at Nain. They introduced European music, including choral traditions, brass bands, and strings. For over two centuries, Nunatsiavut Inuit have maintained this unique tradition — Inuit brass bands play Haydn and Bach on the Labrador coast to this day, combining Inuit voices and European classical repertoire. This is an unusual cultural hybrid unique to Nunatsiavut.

TOONIK TYME AND COMMUNITY FESTIVALS
- Toonik Tyme — an annual spring festival held every April in Iqaluit. Week-long event featuring traditional Inuit games, igloo-building contests, dogsled and snowmobile races, seal-skinning contests, feasts, and music.
- Alianait Arts Festival — Iqaluit's summer music, theatre, film, and arts festival in late June/early July.
- Nattiq Frolics — Taloyoak's spring festival.
- Nunavik Trails — festivals across Nunavik communities.
- Nain Music Festival and similar events in Nunatsiavut communities.

TRADITIONAL GAMES
Traditional Inuit games (pinguarusiit or pingualautiliriniq) test skills tied to Arctic survival:
- Akidlugmik (one-foot high kick) — jumping and kicking a target suspended high in the air, landing on the same foot.
- Ailartiavikluktuq (two-foot high kick) — kicking with both feet together.
- Knuckle hop (pisiffialartiavikluktuq) — hopping on knuckles and toes across the floor.
- Arm pull — two seated players pull against each other by hooked arms.
- Ear pull — two players pull on each other's ears using a strap or string.
- Airplane (body carry) — player held rigid by multiple people and carried as far as possible.
- Blanket toss (nalukataq) — seal or walrus-skin blanket used to toss a person high; historically used to spot game, now a celebratory game.

STORYTELLING AND ORAL TRADITION
Oral storytelling (unikkaaqtuat / unikkaatuat) remains central. Key story cycles include:
- Sedna (also called Nuliajuk, Takannaaluk, or Arnapkapfaaluk in different regions) — the story of a woman whose fingers became the sea mammals; she is guardian of marine creatures and must be respected to ensure hunting success.
- Atanarjuat — an ancient legend about a runner who escapes jealous rivals; the basis of Zacharias Kunuk's film.
- Kaugjagjuk — the orphan boy mistreated by his community who grows into a powerful avenger.
- Stories of Tuniit — giants or earlier people said to have lived in the land before Inuit (often identified with the Dorset archaeological culture).

SPIRITUAL LIFE
Traditional Inuit belief was animistic. Every person, animal, and object was understood to have an inua (spirit). The angakkuq (shaman) mediated between the human world and the spirit world, healing the sick, locating game, and resolving crises. Today most Inuit are Christian — Anglican, Catholic, Moravian (in Nunatsiavut), Pentecostal, and others — but traditional beliefs and IQ principles remain woven through cultural and community life.
