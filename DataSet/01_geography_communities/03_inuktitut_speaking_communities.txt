TOPIC: Inuktitut-Speaking Regions and Communities
SOURCES: Statistics Canada (2021 Census); Inuit Tapiriit Kanatami (ITK); Kativik Regional Government; Nunatsiavut Government; Government of Nunavut; Wikipedia (Inuktitut, Nunavik, Nunatsiavut)

WHO SPEAKS INUKTITUT
Inuktitut (ᐃᓄᒃᑎᑐᑦ), also known as Eastern Canadian Inuktitut, is the most widely spoken Inuit language variety in Canada. It is spoken primarily in:
- Most of Nunavut (all regions except the Kitikmeot west, where Inuinnaqtun is spoken)
- Nunavik, the northern third of Quebec
- Nunatsiavut, on the north coast of Labrador (where the local variety is called Inuttut or Inuttitut)

Important distinction: not all Inuit in Canada are Inuktitut speakers.
- Inuvialuit in the Inuvialuit Settlement Region (NWT) speak Inuvialuktun.
- Inuit in the Kitikmeot region of western Nunavut largely speak Inuinnaqtun.
- Inuktitut, Inuinnaqtun, and Inuvialuktun are related but distinct varieties. Together with Inuttut (Labrador), they are collectively called Inuktut ("Inuit languages").

WHERE INUKTITUT SPEAKERS ARE CONCENTRATED
According to the 2021 Canadian Census:
- 92.2% of Inuktut speakers live within Inuit Nunangat.
- Inuktitut speakers specifically are concentrated in Nunavut (about 60.3% of speakers) and Nunavik (about 30.9%).
- In Nunavut, over 80% of the roughly 24,000–27,000 Inuit speak Inuktitut; about 3,500 are monolingual Inuktitut speakers.
- In Nunavik (Quebec), roughly 80.9% of Quebec Inuit speak Inuktitut.
- In Nunatsiavut, Inuttut is seriously endangered; only a few hundred speakers remain, mostly in Nain.

NUNAVUT — INUKTITUT-SPEAKING COMMUNITIES
Nunavut was created on April 1, 1999, from the eastern portion of the Northwest Territories. It is Canada's largest territory, covering about one-fifth of Canada's land mass. Its capital is Iqaluit. Nunavut has 25 communities (26 including Bathurst Inlet/Umingmaktok), roughly 85% of which is Inuit.

Qikiqtaaluk (Baffin) Region — South and North Baffin Inuktitut dialects:
- Iqaluit — capital; population roughly 7,429 (2021 census). Formerly called Frobisher Bay until 1987. The city's name means "place of many fish" in Inuktitut. About 46% of residents identify Inuktitut as their mother tongue. Iqaluit has the highest Inuit population of any Canadian city over 5,000, and it is Canada's smallest and northernmost capital city.
- Pangnirtung — a community of roughly 1,500 on Baffin Island, known for its tapestry studio and as gateway to Auyuittuq National Park.
- Pond Inlet (Mittimatalik) — northern Baffin; North Qikiqtaaluk dialect; known for narwhal hunting and proximity to Sirmilik National Park.
- Arctic Bay (Ikpiarjuk) — meaning "the pocket" in Inuktitut.
- Cape Dorset (Kinngait) — world-famous centre of Inuit printmaking; roughly 1,300 residents, 95% Inuit.
- Clyde River (Kangiqtugaapik), Qikiqtarjuaq, Igloolik (Iglulik), Hall Beach (Sanirajak), Resolute Bay (Qausuittuq), Grise Fiord (Aujuittuq), Sanikiluaq.

Kivalliq Region — Kivalliq and Aivilik Inuktitut dialects:
- Rankin Inlet (Kangiqliniq) — regional centre, roughly 2,800 people.
- Arviat — roughly 2,657 people in 2016; the southernmost community in Nunavut and one of its most Inuktitut-speaking.
- Baker Lake (Qamani'tuaq) — Nunavut's only inland community.
- Chesterfield Inlet (Igluligaarjuk), Coral Harbour (Salliq), Naujaat (Repulse Bay), Whale Cove (Tikirarjuaq).

Kitikmeot Region — NOT primarily Inuktitut-speaking. Inuinnaqtun is the main Inuit language here. Communities include Cambridge Bay (Iqaluktuuttiaq), Kugluktuk, Gjoa Haven, Taloyoak, Kugaaruk. Note: the Natsilik (Netsilik) dialect spoken around Taloyoak and Kugaaruk is classified by some as an Inuktitut dialect and by others as transitional between Inuktitut and Inuinnaqtun.

NUNAVIK — INUKTITUT-SPEAKING COMMUNITIES
Nunavik covers the northern third of Quebec, above the 55th parallel, and is part of the wider Inuit Nunangat. The James Bay and Northern Quebec Agreement of 1975 was Canada's first modern comprehensive land claim. The region is administered by the Kativik Regional Government (KRG), based in Kuujjuaq. The Makivik Corporation, also headquartered in Kuujjuaq, represents Nunavik Inuit politically and economically.

Population: 14,045 (2021 census), of whom about 90% are Inuit (Nunavimmiut). "Nunavik" means "great land" in the local Inuktitut dialect, Nunavimmiutut (ᐃᓄᑦᑎᑐᑦ), sometimes called Tarramiutut or Taqramiutut.

The 14 communities of Nunavik, along the Hudson Bay, Hudson Strait, and Ungava Bay coasts:
- Akulivik
- Aupaluk
- Inukjuak (population around 1,800; the region's cultural and literary centre; home to the Avataq Cultural Institute)
- Ivujivik
- Kangiqsualujjuaq (birthplace of Governor General Mary Simon)
- Kangiqsujuaq
- Kangirsuk
- Kuujjuaq (regional administrative capital; population approximately 1,800, the largest community in Nunavik)
- Kuujjuarapik
- Puvirnituq (population roughly 1,800; major regional hospital)
- Quaqtaq
- Salluit
- Tasiujaq
- Umiujaq

All communities are reachable only by air or sealift; no roads connect them.

NUNATSIAVUT — INUTTUT/INUTTITUT-SPEAKING COMMUNITIES
Nunatsiavut (ᓄᓇᑦᓯᐊᕗᑦ) means "Our Beautiful Land" in Inuttut. It is the autonomous Inuit region of northern Labrador and the first Inuit region in Canada to achieve self-government (December 1, 2005). The settlement area covers 72,520 km² of land plus 48,690 km² of ocean.

The five Nunatsiavut communities:
- Nain — administrative capital; largest community (population around 1,100); Inuttut is still spoken here by a small number of residents.
- Hopedale — legislative capital; site of the historic Hopedale Mission Complex (founded 1782).
- Makkovik
- Postville
- Rigolet — the southernmost officially recognized Inuit community in Canada.

Governance: the Nunatsiavut Government has five Inuit Community Governments, one per community. Each is led by an AngajukKâk ("chief executive officer and mayor"), who must be Inuk. The Nunatsiavut Assembly sits in Hopedale; the administrative head office is in Nain.

Nunatsiavut Inuttut uses a distinct writing system developed in Greenland in the 1760s by German Moravian missionaries, including the letter "K" (kra) for a specific uvular sound.

INUKTITUT-SPEAKING DIASPORA
Beyond Inuit Nunangat, there are significant Inuktitut-speaking populations in southern cities. Ottawa has a particularly notable community — over 3,000 Inuit, largely from Nunavut and Nunavik, supported by organizations like Ottawa Inuit Children's Centre and Tungasuvvingat Inuit.

In Canada as a whole, about 7.8% of Inuktut speakers live outside Inuit Nunangat; of this group, 95.5% are Inuktitut speakers specifically.
