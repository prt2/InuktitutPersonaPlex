TOPIC: Food, Daily Life, and Housing in Inuktitut-Speaking Communities
SOURCES: The Canadian Encyclopedia; Wikipedia (Inuit cuisine); Britannica; ITK; Adventure Canada; Nunavut Food Security Coalition; Feeding My Family movement

COUNTRY FOOD (NIQITUINNAQ)
"Niqituinnaq" or more broadly "niqi" is the Inuktitut word for traditional foods harvested from the land, sea, and ice. The word "country food" is used in English. In Nunavut and Nunavik, country food remains central to diet, identity, and community — especially given the extraordinary cost of imported groceries (often 2–3 times southern Canadian prices).

MAJOR TRADITIONAL FOODS

Sea mammals (most important category):
- Nattiq (ᓇᑦᓯᖅ) — ringed seal; the staple sea mammal of most Inuktitut-speaking communities.
- Ugjuk (ᐅᒡᔪᒃ) — bearded seal.
- Aiviq (ᐊᐃᕕᖅ) — walrus; harvested in Nunavik and eastern Nunavut, sometimes fermented as igunaq.
- Qilalugaq qaqurtaq (ᕿᓚᓗᒐᖅ ᖃᖁᕐᑕᖅ) — beluga whale.
- Qilalugaq tugaalik (ᕿᓚᓗᒐᖅ ᑐᒐᓕᒃ) — narwhal.
- Arviq (ᐊᕐᕕᖅ) — bowhead whale; a single bowhead can feed an entire community for close to a year.

Land mammals:
- Tuktu (ᑐᒃᑐ) — caribou; the most important land mammal. Different caribou herds supply different communities (George River, Leaf River, Qamanirjuaq, Beverly).
- Umingmak (ᐅᒥᖕᒪᒃ) — muskox.
- Nanuq (ᓇᓄᖅ) — polar bear; hunted under tightly regulated tag systems.
- Ukaliq (ᐅᑲᓕᖅ) — arctic hare.

Fish:
- Iqaluk (ᐃᖃᓗᒃ) — Arctic char; the most prized fish; its plural "iqaluit" gives Nunavut's capital its name.
- Uluakik — Arctic cod.
- Ikalukpik — lake trout.
- Kapisilik — capelin (Nunavik).

Birds and eggs:
- Aqiggiq — ptarmigan.
- Mitiq — eider duck; eggs are important seasonal food.
- Kanguq — snow goose.

Plants and berries (gathered mainly late summer through early autumn):
- Aqpik (ᐊᕐᐱᒃ) — cloudberry (bakeapple); highly prized.
- Kigutangirnat — crowberry.
- Kimminait — mountain cranberry / lingonberry.
- Paurngait — blackberry / blueberry (varies by dialect).
- Seaweed, mountain sorrel, tundra roots.

TRADITIONAL PREPARATIONS AND DISHES

- Quaq (ᖁᐊᖅ) — frozen raw meat or fish, thinly sliced. Simple, quick, highly nutritious.
- Pitsik / pipsi — dried fish (Arctic char, trout), often air-dried on racks in summer.
- Misiraq — aged or fermented seal fat or blubber oil; used as a dipping condiment.
- Igunaq (ᐃᒍᓇᖅ) — fermented walrus or seal meat; traditional delicacy, requires careful preparation.
- Muktuk / mattaaq — whale skin and blubber, eaten raw, frozen, or boiled. An important source of vitamin C.
- Suaasat (Nunavik/Nunatsiavut crossover) — boiled meat soup (seal, whale, caribou, or seabirds) with broth, often with barley, onions, and potatoes in modern versions.
- Nikku — dried caribou meat.
- Bannock (pitsi or paluak) — fried or baked bread introduced via European contact; now ubiquitous in Inuit households.
- Akutaq — "Inuit ice cream," traditionally whipped animal fat mixed with berries, snow, and sometimes small bits of fish.

HUNTING AND SEASONAL ROUND

Spring (silamut aningajuq):
- Seal hunting at breathing holes (mauliq) or at cracks in sea ice.
- Goose and bird egg hunting.
- Char begin running back to the sea from lakes.

Summer (aujaq):
- Fishing for Arctic char at weirs (saputi) as fish migrate.
- Caribou hunting in some regions.
- Berry picking in August–September.
- Walrus and whale hunting in open water.

Autumn (ukiaq):
- Caribou hunting during migration; when snowfall begins, tracking is easier.
- Char freeze-up fishing.
- Preparation and preservation of meat for winter.

Winter (ukiuq):
- Seal hunting at breathing holes (skill known as "maupok" hunting — waiting patiently with a harpoon).
- Ice fishing.
- Trapping fox and other fur-bearers.

MODERN TOOLS
Hunters today use rifles, snowmobiles (skidoos), ATVs, and motorboats while retaining traditional tracking, butchering, and preservation skills. Harpoons remain essential for seal and whale hunting on ice.

FOOD SECURITY
Food insecurity is a major issue. Nunavut has the highest food insecurity rate in Canada — roughly 57% of Inuit households in Nunavut experience food insecurity (Statistics Canada). Drivers include:
- Extreme cost of imported food (a case of water can cost $100; a gallon of milk $15–$20).
- Climate change affecting ice travel and game availability.
- Decline of traditional skills among some youth.

Responses include:
- Nutrition North Canada — federal food subsidy program (launched 2011, under constant reform).
- Hunter support programs in Nunavut and Nunavik that compensate hunters for country food shared with communities.
- Community freezers and harvester programs.
- Feeding My Family movement — founded by Leesee Papatsie (Iqaluit) in 2012 to raise awareness about food prices.
- Niqihaqut / country food distribution programs.

CLOTHING

Atigi (ᐊᑎᒋ) — inner parka, traditionally caribou-fur with the fur against the body for insulation.
Sikupak — outer parka.
Amauti (ᐊᒪᐅᑎ, plural amautiit) — women's parka with a pouch on the back for carrying a baby; unique design that keeps the infant warm against the mother while leaving her arms free. Iconic Inuit garment.
Kamiik (ᑲᒥᒃ, singular kamik) — warm sealskin or caribou boots, hand-stitched with sinew.
Qarliik — trousers, traditionally caribou.
Qiviut — the soft underwool of the muskox; prized for knitting. Eight times warmer than sheep's wool by weight.

Today many community members sew high-quality winter clothing that blends traditional designs with modern materials like down, nylon, and fur trim.

HOUSING

Traditional shelter forms:
- Iglu (ᐃᒡᓗ) — snow-block dome winter house. "Iglu" is the Inuktitut word for "house" generally; modern usage in English uses "igloo" specifically for the snow-block structure. Builds use spiral-cut snow blocks; a well-built iglu is thermally efficient — the interior can be 20–40°C warmer than outside temperatures.
- Qarmaq (ᖃᕐᒪᖅ) — semi-subterranean winter house built of stone, sod, whalebone, and driftwood framing; used before permanent modern housing.
- Tupiq (ᑐᐱᖅ) — skin tent used in summer.

Today, Inuit live in modern insulated housing in permanent communities. Housing shortages and severe overcrowding are chronic problems in many communities, with families sharing small houses in two or three shifts. Makivik Corporation, the Kativik Municipal Housing Bureau, Nunavut Housing Corporation, and Nunatsiavut Government administer ongoing construction programs.

TRANSPORTATION

Qamutik (ᖃᒧᑏᒃ) — traditional sled, often 4–6 metres long, with wooden or bone runners. Historically pulled by sled dogs; today more often towed by snowmobile.
Qajaq (ᖃᔭᖅ) — one-person skin-covered hunting kayak. The English word "kayak" comes directly from Inuktitut. Mostly replaced by motorboats, though qajaq-building is being revived as a cultural skill.
Umiaq (ᐅᒥᐊᖅ) — large, open skin boat; used historically for whale hunting and transporting families.
Qimmiq (ᕿᒻᒥᖅ) — Inuit sled dog; the Canadian Eskimo Dog is a distinct breed adapted for Arctic conditions. After the mid-20th-century sled dog killings, populations were devastated; recovery efforts are ongoing (notably in Mittimatalik/Pond Inlet).

Today:
- Snowmobiles are ubiquitous.
- All-terrain vehicles (ATVs or honda, as many Inuit call them colloquially) are used in summer.
- Motorboats for summer travel on water.
- Air travel is the main way to reach most communities from the south — Canadian North, First Air, Air Inuit, and regional carriers connect Nunavut and Nunavik communities.
- Sealift — once or twice per year, supply ships deliver bulk goods (vehicles, building materials, non-perishables) during the open-water season.

No roads connect any Inuktitut-speaking community to the southern Canadian highway network, except for the Labrador Inuit town of Happy Valley-Goose Bay area (which is adjacent to Nunatsiavut but outside Nunatsiavut communities proper).
