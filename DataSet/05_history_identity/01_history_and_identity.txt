TOPIC: History and Identity
SOURCES: Inuit Tapiriit Kanatami; The Canadian Encyclopedia; Indigenous Peoples Atlas of Canada; Crown-Indigenous Relations and Northern Affairs Canada

ORIGINS AND EARLY HISTORY
Inuit trace their origins to the Thule culture, who migrated eastward across the Arctic from Alaska roughly 1,000 years ago, gradually replacing the earlier Dorset culture. Thule ancestors themselves descended from earlier Arctic Small Tool tradition and Paleo-Inuit peoples, making Arctic settlement a cultural continuity spanning about 5,000 years. By roughly 1100 CE, Thule migrations had reached Greenland.

Inuit are culturally and linguistically distinct from First Nations and Métis peoples. Under sections 25 and 35 of the Canadian Constitution Act of 1982, Inuit are recognized as one of three distinct Indigenous peoples of Canada.

CONTACT AND COLONIAL PERIOD
- Norse contact in Greenland and parts of the eastern Arctic began around 1000 CE.
- Sustained European contact with Canadian Inuit began in the 1500s–1600s through whalers, explorers, and Hudson's Bay Company traders.
- Christian missions (Moravian in Labrador from the 1770s, later Anglican and Catholic across the Arctic) introduced writing systems, schools, and major cultural changes.

20TH CENTURY DISRUPTIONS
Several 20th-century federal policies had devastating effects on Inuit communities:
- Forced relocations (1950s) — families were moved to High Arctic sites including Grise Fiord and Resolute Bay, in part to assert Canadian sovereignty. The Government of Canada formally apologized in 2010.
- Residential schools and federal day schools — Inuit children were taken from their families, punished for speaking Inuktut, and separated from cultural practices.
- The tuberculosis epidemic (peaking 1940s–1960s) saw many Inuit sent south for treatment, often without proper communication to families; some never returned and were buried far from home. The federal Nanilavut Initiative now helps families locate loved ones.
- The Inuit "disc number" system (E-numbers) was imposed from the 1940s to 1970s in place of recording Inuit names — Project Surname (led by Abe Okpik in the early 1970s) restored family names.
- Sled dog killings — between the 1950s and 1970s, thousands of sled dogs (qimmiit) were killed by RCMP and other authorities, undermining mobility and livelihood. Both the Quebec and Canadian governments have since acknowledged and investigated this harm.

LAND CLAIMS AND SELF-GOVERNMENT
Modern Inuit political history is defined by land claim agreements and the establishment of self-governing regions:
- 1975: James Bay and Northern Quebec Agreement (Nunavik).
- 1984: Inuvialuit Final Agreement (Inuvialuit Settlement Region).
- 1993: Nunavut Land Claims Agreement.
- 1999: Creation of the Territory of Nunavut.
- 2005: Labrador Inuit Land Claims Agreement (Nunatsiavut).

NATIONAL REPRESENTATION
Inuit Tapiriit Kanatami (ITK), founded in 1971 (originally as Inuit Tapirisat of Canada), is the national representational organization for Inuit in Canada. It advocates on rights, health, education, language, and policy, representing the four land-claim regions and the National Inuit Youth Council.

IDENTITY TODAY
Inuit identity today combines deep continuity — language, country food, kinship ties, knowledge of the land — with contemporary participation in Canadian political and economic life. Inuit culture, sometimes declared "vanishing" by outside observers in the 20th century, is demonstrably alive: active use of Inuktut, thriving art and music, emerging Inuit-led film and media (Atanarjuat: The Fast Runner, 2001, was the first feature film written, produced, directed, and acted in Inuktitut), and strong national and regional institutions.

Notable Inuit individuals include:
- Abe Okpik — led Project Surname.
- Mary Simon — appointed Governor General of Canada in 2021, the first Indigenous person to hold the role.
- Susan Aglukark — Juno Award-winning singer-songwriter.
- Kenojuak Ashevak and Pitseolak Ashoona — internationally celebrated artists.
- Zacharias Kunuk — filmmaker (Atanarjuat).
- Tanya Tagaq — throat singer and Polaris Prize winner.
- Sheila Watt-Cloutier — Nobel Peace Prize nominee for environmental and human rights advocacy.
