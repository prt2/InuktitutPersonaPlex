TOPIC: Inuktitut Language Status, Legislation, and Revitalization
SOURCES: Inuit Uqausinginnik Taiguusiliuqtiit; Government of Nunavut; Nunavut Education Act; Inuit Language Protection Act; James Bay and Northern Quebec Agreement

OFFICIAL STATUS

Nunavut:
- Inuktitut is an official language alongside Inuinnaqtun (together called "Inuit Language" or Inuktut), English, and French.
- Inuit Language Protection Act (2008) — grants every parent the right to have their children receive Inuit Language instruction; requires the Government of Nunavut to produce secondary school graduates fully proficient in the Inuit Language. Full primary-through-secondary implementation was originally scheduled for July 1, 2019.
- Official Languages Act (2008, amended 2013) — recognizes Inuktut as an official language and calls for revitalizing its use in education, work, and daily life.
- Nunavut Education Act (2008) — requires Inuit Societal Values and Inuit Qaujimajatuqangit principles to be incorporated throughout the education system.

Northwest Territories:
- Inuktitut is one of eight official Indigenous languages (alongside English and French).

Nunavik (Quebec):
- Inuktitut is not provincially official, but has legal recognition through the James Bay and Northern Quebec Agreement (1975).
- Under Quebec's Charter of the French Language, Inuktitut is the official language of instruction for Inuit school districts in Nunavik.
- Kativik Ilisarniliriniq (the Nunavik school board, formerly Kativik School Board) uses Inuktitut as a primary language of instruction in early grades.

Nunatsiavut (Labrador):
- Inuttut is the official government language of Nunatsiavut since its 2005 establishment.

LANGUAGE INSTITUTIONS

Inuit Uqausinginnik Taiguusiliuqtiit (IUT) — the Inuit Language Authority established under the Inuit Language Protection Act; tasked with expanding knowledge and standardization of Inuktut. IUT adopted "Inuktut" in 2015 as the umbrella term for all Canadian Inuit languages.

Pirurvik Centre — Iqaluit-based Inuktitut language training centre. Developed the online resource Inuktut Tusaalanga (tusaalanga.ca), which offers lessons in multiple dialects. Works to train Inuktitut instructors from across Nunavut.

Avataq Cultural Institute — Nunavik-based; documents and preserves Nunavik Inuktitut (Nunavimmiutut), runs dictionaries, and archives oral history.

Kativik Ilisarniliriniq — Nunavik school board that manages education in all 14 Nunavik communities with Inuktitut-language instruction in early grades.

Torngâsok Cultural Centre — Nunatsiavut; preserves and promotes Inuttut.

NATIONAL STRATEGY
Inuit Tapiriit Kanatami (ITK) released the National Inuit Strategy on Inuit Education (2011) and has pushed for federal recognition of Inuktut as a founding language of Canada. Under the federal Indigenous Languages Act (2019), Canada, Nunavut, and Nunavut Tunngavik Incorporated have worked toward a Revitalizing and Strengthening Inuktut in Nunavut agreement; the federal and territorial governments committed roughly $42 million and $25 million respectively over five years.

REVITALIZATION CHALLENGES
- Teacher shortage: A 2016 Office of the Languages Commissioner of Nunavut report found that only 11 of 27 schools were able to offer Inuktitut-language courses from Kindergarten to Grade 3.
- Declining fluency among youth: while adults often retain Inuktitut fluency, younger speakers in many communities are more proficient in English.
- Media and technology: efforts include CBC North (Inuktitut radio and television programming), APTN, Nunavut-produced film (Isuma Productions), and digital tools like the Tusaalanga app.

WRITING AND STANDARDIZATION

In 2019, Inuit Tapiriit Kanatami adopted Inuktut Qaliujaaqpait, a unified Roman-orthography system designed to work across Canadian Inuit dialects. It was developed over six years by the Atausiq Inuktut Titirausiq task force. Syllabics remain widely used and are not replaced by Qaliujaaqpait — the new system is meant to complement syllabics and make cross-dialect writing easier.

Two traditional writing systems persist side-by-side:
1. Syllabics (qaniujaaqpait) — used in Nunavut (east of Kitikmeot) and Nunavik. Adapted from Cree syllabics in the 1800s by missionaries John Horden, E. A. Watkins, and Edmund Peck.
2. Roman orthography — used in Nunatsiavut (Moravian system, unique to the region), Kitikmeot (Inuinnaqtun), and by ITK for cross-regional work.

AJUINNATA — A KEY INUKTITUT WORD
Governor General Mary Simon (herself from Kangiqsualujjuaq, Nunavik) has highlighted ajuinnata as a guiding concept. The word does not have a direct one-word English translation — it carries the meaning of a vow or promise never to give up, a commitment to push forward no matter how difficult. Simon says the word was taught to her by her mother and grandmother.
