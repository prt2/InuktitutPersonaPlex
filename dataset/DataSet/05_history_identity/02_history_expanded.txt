TOPIC: History of Inuktitut-Speaking Regions
SOURCES: The Canadian Encyclopedia; Indigenous Peoples Atlas of Canada; Parks Canada; Inuit Tapiriit Kanatami; Nunatsiavut Government; Avataq Cultural Institute

ANCIENT HISTORY (PRE-1200 CE)

Paleo-Inuit / Pre-Dorset — people migrated from Siberia across the Bering Strait beginning around 4,500–5,000 years ago, eventually reaching as far east as Greenland. The first known Paleo-Inuit culture in what is now Nunavut developed around 2500 BCE.

Dorset culture (c. 500 BCE to 1000–1500 CE) — named for Cape Dorset (now Kinngait), where archaeological evidence was first identified in 1925 by anthropologist Diamond Jenness. The Dorset hunted mainly marine mammals, used soapstone oil lamps (early qulliq), and produced distinctive ivory and stone art. They lacked bow-and-arrow and drill technology. Inuit oral tradition remembers earlier people called Tuniit or Sivullirmiut ("first inhabitants"), often described as gentle giants — widely believed to refer to the Dorset.

Thule culture / Early Inuit — the direct ancestors of today's Inuit. Around 1200 CE, Thule groups migrated rapidly eastward from Alaska across what is now Nunavut and Nunavik, reaching Greenland by the 13th century. Thule technology was significantly more sophisticated than the preceding Dorset: bows and arrows, dog sleds, large skin boats (umiat), one-person kayaks (qajait), sophisticated harpoons, bow drills, and use of native copper and iron (some iron sourced from the Cape York meteorites in northwest Greenland and some via trade with Norse Greenlanders).

Within a few centuries of Thule arrival, Dorset culture disappeared entirely. Genetic studies show Dorset and Thule were distinct populations with little genetic mixing; the Dorset seem to have been displaced, not assimilated. The Sadlermiut of Southampton Island, a distinctive isolated group that survived until 1902–1903, may have been the last remnant Dorset culture.

The Thule-to-Inuit transition is understood to have occurred between roughly 1600 and 1850, during the Little Ice Age (c. 1400–1850). Colder conditions reduced open water hunting of bowhead whales, causing many Thule to abandon large whaling settlements and develop the more mobile lifestyle Europeans would later encounter.

CONTACT AND EARLY COLONIAL PERIOD

Norse contact (c. 1000 CE) — the first documented European contact with Thule peoples in Arctic Canada and Greenland. The Norse sagas refer to Inuit (and sometimes the Dorset) as "Skrælings." There is archaeological evidence of limited trade and conflict.

Martin Frobisher (1576–1578) — English explorer who named Frobisher Bay (site of modern Iqaluit) while searching for the Northwest Passage and gold.

Moravian missionaries in Labrador (from 1771) — German-speaking Moravians established the first permanent mission at Nain in 1771, followed by Okak (1776), Hopedale (1782), Hebron (1831), and Makkovik (1896). They created the Inuttut writing system based on a Greenlandic precedent (Moravians had started working in Greenland in 1733). They brought European trade goods, literacy, schooling, and musical traditions (Haydn, Bach, brass bands), but also disease epidemics that devastated Labrador Inuit populations.

Hudson's Bay Company posts (from the 1800s–1920s) — established trading posts across Nunavut and Nunavik, drawing Inuit into the fur-trade economy. Examples: Kuujjuaq (Fort Chimo), Inukjuak (Port Harrison), Kinngait (Cape Dorset, post established 1913), Iqaluit region (trade networks from the 1600s, post establishments later).

Anglican and Catholic missions — followed the Moravians into Nunavut and Nunavik during the late 1800s and early 1900s. Anglican missionary Edmund Peck, working with E. A. Watkins and John Horden, adapted Cree syllabics for Inuktitut in the 1870s–1880s; Peck translated portions of the Bible.

20TH CENTURY — SETTLEMENT AND COLONIAL POLICIES

Tuberculosis crisis (peaked 1940s–1960s) — hundreds of Inuit were sent thousands of kilometres south to sanatoriums, often without adequate communication to families. Many died and were buried in unmarked graves far from home. The federal Nanilavut Initiative (launched 2019) helps families locate relatives' burial sites.

"Disc number" system / Project Surname — from the 1940s, the federal government issued Inuit "E-numbers" (Eskimo-number identification discs) worn around the neck, because officials found Inuit names difficult. Abe Okpik led Project Surname (1968–1970), which restored family surnames across Nunavut and Nunavik.

High Arctic relocations (1953–1955) — the Canadian government relocated Inuit families from Inukjuak in Nunavik and Mittimatalik in Qikiqtaaluk to Grise Fiord and Resolute Bay, in part to assert Canadian sovereignty in the High Arctic. Families faced extreme hardship. The Government of Canada issued a formal apology in 2010.

Nutaraqnait / Sled dog killings (1950s–1970s) — Royal Canadian Mounted Police and other authorities killed thousands of sled dogs (qimmiit) across Nunavik and Nunavut, undermining Inuit mobility and traditional life. The Quebec and federal governments have since acknowledged and investigated the harm. A commemorative settlement for Nunavik was reached in 2019 and for Nunavut (through Qikiqtani Truth Commission) in 2019–2020.

Residential schools and federal day schools — from the 1890s but especially 1950s–1970s, Inuit children across Nunavut, Nunavik, and Nunatsiavut were taken from their families, sometimes to distant schools. Students were punished for speaking Inuktitut. Major schools included Sir Joseph Bernier Federal Day School (Chesterfield Inlet), Churchill Vocational Centre (Manitoba), and Moravian and Anglican boarding schools in Labrador. The federal government issued a formal apology in 2008, and Mary Simon delivered the Inuit response in the House of Commons.

Qikiqtani Truth Commission (2008–2013) — an Inuit-led commission investigating federal actions against Inuit in the Qikiqtaaluk region from 1950 to 1975, including sled dog killings, relocations, and disruption of traditional life.

LAND CLAIMS AND SELF-GOVERNMENT

1975 — James Bay and Northern Quebec Agreement — Canada's first modern comprehensive land claim agreement, signed by Inuit of Nunavik (through the Northern Quebec Inuit Association, later Makivik Corporation), the Grand Council of the Crees, Quebec, and Canada. Triggered by the James Bay hydroelectric project. Established the Kativik Regional Government (KRG), the Kativik School Board, and the Nunavik Regional Board of Health and Social Services.

1993 — Nunavut Land Claims Agreement — the largest Indigenous land claim settlement in Canadian history. Signed by Inuit of the eastern Northwest Territories (represented by Tungavik Federation of Nunavut, now Nunavut Tunngavik Incorporated), Canada, and the NWT government. Cleared the path for Nunavut's creation.

April 1, 1999 — Nunavut created — formally separated from the Northwest Territories. Iqaluit selected as capital (over Rankin Inlet) in a 1995 plebiscite. Nunavut uses a consensus-style, non-party legislature.

2005 — Labrador Inuit Land Claims Agreement — signed December 1, 2005; established the Nunatsiavut Government, the first Inuit region in Canada with full self-government provisions. The agreement covers 72,520 km² of land and 48,690 km² of ocean.

2008 — federal apology for residential schools; Inuit Language Protection Act and Official Languages Act passed in Nunavut.

2011 — Mary Simon published the National Committee on Inuit Education report; ITK released the National Strategy on Inuit Education.

2017 — Inuit Nunangat Policy announced by the federal government, committing to apply distinctions-based approaches to Inuit across the four land-claim regions.

2019 — Indigenous Languages Act passed federally; ITK adopted Inuktut Qaliujaaqpait unified Roman orthography.

2021 — Mary Simon (from Kangiqsualujjuaq, Nunavik) became the 30th Governor General of Canada and the first Indigenous person to hold the role.

KEY REPRESENTATIONAL ORGANIZATIONS

Inuit Tapiriit Kanatami (ITK) — national; founded 1971 as Inuit Tapirisat of Canada.
Nunavut Tunngavik Incorporated (NTI) — represents Inuit of Nunavut under the land claims agreement; based in Iqaluit.
Qikiqtani Inuit Association (QIA), Kivalliq Inuit Association (KIA), Kitikmeot Inuit Association (KitIA) — regional Inuit associations under NTI.
Makivik Corporation (Makivvik) — represents Nunavik Inuit; headquartered in Kuujjuaq.
Nunatsiavut Government — self-governing Inuit regional government in Labrador; headquartered in Nain.
Pauktuutit Inuit Women of Canada — national advocacy organization for Inuit women and girls; founded 1984, based in Ottawa.
Inuit Circumpolar Council (ICC) — represents Inuit in Canada, Greenland, Alaska, and Chukotka; founded 1977.
