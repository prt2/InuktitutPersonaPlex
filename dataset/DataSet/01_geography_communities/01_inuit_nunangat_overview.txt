TOPIC: Geography and Communities — Inuit Nunangat Overview
SOURCES: Inuit Tapiriit Kanatami (ITK); Indigenous Peoples Atlas of Canada; Statistics Canada; Crown-Indigenous Relations and Northern Affairs Canada

Inuit Nunangat is the Inuit homeland in Canada. The term is a Canadian Inuit term that includes land, water, and ice — Inuit consider all three integral to their culture and way of life.

Inuit Nunangat is made up of four regions:
1. Inuvialuit Settlement Region — northern Northwest Territories (and historically extending into northern Yukon).
2. Nunavut — Canada's largest and newest territory, created in 1999. Capital: Iqaluit.
3. Nunavik — the northern third of Quebec.
4. Nunatsiavut — the northern coast of Labrador.

Key facts:
- Inuit Nunangat encompasses roughly 35% of Canada's land mass and 50% of its coastline.
- There are 53 communities across Inuit Nunangat (the Government of Canada includes Bathurst Inlet, Killiniq, and Umingmaktuuq/Umingmaktok in this count).
- The majority of the approximately 65,000 Inuit in Canada live within Inuit Nunangat. Roughly one-quarter live outside, with about half of those in towns or cities.
- Nunavut alone is home to around 25,000 Inuit across 26 communities.
- Nunavut is divided into three administrative regions: Qikiqtaaluk (east, Baffin region), Kivalliq (central Arctic, western Hudson Bay coast), and Kitikmeot (west).

Inuit co-manage their homelands with federal, provincial, and territorial governments through constitutionally protected land claims agreements.

Inuit history in this region spans roughly 5,000 years, with cultural and linguistic continuity stretching from Chukotka (Russia) across Alaska and Canada to southeastern Greenland.
