TOPIC: Culture and Traditions
SOURCES: Inuit Tapiriit Kanatami; The Canadian Encyclopedia; Indigenous Peoples Atlas of Canada; Government of Nunavut

INUIT QAUJIMAJATUQANGIT (IQ)
Inuit Qaujimajatuqangit, often shortened to "IQ," is the body of traditional Inuit knowledge, values, and worldview. The phrase roughly translates to "that which Inuit have long known." It includes environmental knowledge, social values, and principles for living well. The Government of Nunavut formally integrates IQ principles into policy. The eight guiding IQ principles are:
1. Inuuqatigiitsiarniq — respecting others, relationships, and caring for people.
2. Tunnganarniq — fostering a welcoming, open, and inclusive environment.
3. Pijitsirniq — serving and providing for family and community.
4. Aajiiqatigiinniq — decision-making through discussion and consensus.
5. Pilimmaksarniq / Pijariuqsarniq — development of skills through observation, mentoring, practice.
6. Piliriqatigiinniq / Ikajuqtigiinniq — working together for a common cause.
7. Qanuqtuurniq — being innovative and resourceful.
8. Avatittinnik Kamatsiarniq — respect and care for the land, animals, and environment.

QULLIQ (SEAL-OIL LAMP)
The qulliq is a crescent-shaped stone lamp, traditionally carved from soapstone, that burns rendered seal or whale oil with an Arctic cotton wick. For thousands of years it was the most essential household object — a single qulliq provided light, heat, a way to dry clothes, and a cooking flame through the long polar winter. It was maintained primarily by women. Today the qulliq is lit at ceremonial openings, government events, and cultural gatherings as a powerful symbol of Inuit identity, continuity, and resilience.

THROAT SINGING (KATAJJAQ)
Katajjaq is a traditional vocal practice typically performed by two women facing each other closely, producing rhythmic, alternating sounds through voiced and unvoiced inhalation and exhalation. It was historically a game and a form of entertainment during long periods when men were away hunting. The performance ends when one singer laughs or runs out of breath. Artists such as Tanya Tagaq have brought the practice to international audiences.

DRUM DANCING
Inuit drum dancing features a large, flat, frame drum (qilaut) struck on the rim rather than the head. The drum is often accompanied by ayaya songs — personal or communal songs passed down through generations or composed for specific occasions. Drum dancing was suppressed during the mission era in many regions and has undergone strong revitalization.

ART AND CRAFT
Inuit art is internationally renowned:
- Stone carving (soapstone, serpentine) — often depicting animals, hunters, spirits, and scenes of daily life. Kinngait (Cape Dorset) is a world-famous art centre.
- Printmaking — began in Cape Dorset in the late 1950s and became a major cultural and economic force.
- Inuit tattoos (kakiniit and tunniit) — traditional women's tattoos, banned for generations under colonial and missionary pressure, are now being revived, notably through the Inuit Tattoo Revitalization Project led by Angela Hovak Johnston.
- Sewing and beadwork — amautiit, parkas, kamiik, wall hangings.

STORYTELLING AND ORAL TRADITION
Oral storytelling is central. Stories include origin narratives (such as the story of Sedna, the sea goddess), hero tales, teaching stories, and family histories. Stories transmit values, survival knowledge, and cultural memory across generations.

GAMES AND SPORTS
Traditional Inuit games test strength, agility, pain tolerance, and mental focus — skills tied to survival in the Arctic. Events like the Arctic Winter Games feature disciplines including:
- One-foot high kick and two-foot high kick
- Knuckle hop
- Arm pull
- Ear pull
- Airplane (body carry)
- Blanket toss (nalukataq) — historically used to spot game from a height; now a celebratory practice.

SPIRITUAL AND CEREMONIAL LIFE
Traditional Inuit belief was animistic — every living and non-living being had a spirit (inua). Angakkuit (shamans/healers) mediated between the human and spirit worlds. Hunting included rituals of respect for the animal's spirit. While most Inuit today are Christian (largely Anglican, Catholic, and Moravian depending on region), traditional beliefs and practices continue to be woven into cultural and community life.
