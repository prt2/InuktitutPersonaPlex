TOPIC: Food and Daily Life
SOURCES: The Canadian Encyclopedia; Wikipedia (Inuit cuisine); Britannica; NPR; Nunavut Food Security Coalition

COUNTRY FOOD
"Country food" is the Inuit term for traditional foods harvested from the land, sea, and ice. It includes game meats, migratory birds, fish, and foraged foods. Country food remains central to Inuit identity, nutrition, and social life — especially in regions where store-bought food is expensive and often limited in variety.

MAIN FOOD SOURCES
- Sea mammals: ringed seal and bearded seal (core staples), walrus, beluga whale, narwhal, bowhead whale. A single bowhead can feed an entire community for close to a year.
- Land mammals: caribou, muskox, polar bear, arctic hare.
- Fish: Arctic char (especially prized), Arctic cod, capelin, lake trout, sculpin.
- Birds and eggs: snow goose, ptarmigan, eider duck, and their eggs.
- Foraged plants: cloudberries (aqpik), crowberries, blueberries, bearberries, mountain sorrel, seaweed.

NOTABLE DISHES AND PREPARATIONS
- Muktuk — skin and blubber of whale (typically bowhead, beluga, or narwhal), eaten raw, frozen, boiled, or pickled. High in vitamin C.
- Suaasat — traditional soup made from seal, whale, caribou, or seabirds.
- Pitsik / pipsi — dried fish (often Arctic char).
- Quaq — frozen raw meat or fish, sliced and eaten directly.
- Igunaq — fermented meat (e.g., walrus). A traditional preparation requiring careful technique.
- Akutaq — a whipped "Arctic ice cream" made from animal fat, berries, and snow.
- Bannock — a bread (originally introduced via contact) now commonly baked over stove or an open flame; sometimes over a qulliq (seal-oil lamp).

PREPARATION METHODS
Raw, frozen, aged, fermented, smoked, and dried preparations are all traditional. These methods both preserve food and give it distinctive flavor. Because the Arctic environment limits agriculture, the diet is traditionally low in carbohydrates and high in fat and animal protein — raw and fresh-frozen meat also provides vitamins (notably vitamin C from whale skin and raw organs) that would otherwise be unavailable.

HUNTING AND SEASONALITY
- Spring/Summer: seal hunting on sea ice, fishing for Arctic char, gathering berries and greens.
- Autumn/Winter: caribou and muskox hunting (snow makes tracking easier), seal hunting through breathing holes.
- Modern tools (rifles, snowmobiles, motorboats) now supplement traditional techniques, but harvesting practices and seasonal rhythms remain largely unchanged.

SHARING AND SOCIAL ROLE OF FOOD
Food sharing is one of the strongest customs in Inuit culture. Hunters share their catch with extended family, elders, and anyone in the community who needs food. This practice strengthens social bonds and provides an informal food-security safety net. Community feasts remain central to celebrations and seasonal events.

MODERN CHALLENGES
- High cost of imported store food in the North means country food is often more affordable as well as more culturally valued.
- Climate change is altering sea ice, migration patterns, and harvesting seasons.
- Younger generations face challenges in learning traditional harvesting skills, though community-led programs actively teach these skills.
