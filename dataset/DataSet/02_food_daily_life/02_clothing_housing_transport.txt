TOPIC: Daily Life — Clothing, Housing, Transport
SOURCES: Britannica; Indigenous Peoples Atlas of Canada; Crown-Indigenous Relations and Northern Affairs Canada

CLOTHING
Traditional Inuit clothing was designed around survival in extreme cold. Caribou fur was the preferred material for winter clothing — the hollow guard hairs provide exceptional insulation. Sealskin was used for waterproof garments, boots, and summer wear.

- Amauti — a traditional women's parka with a built-in pouch at the back for carrying an infant, keeping the baby warm against the mother's skin.
- Kamiik (or mukluks) — warm, flexible boots made of sealskin or caribou, often with decorative trim.
- Qiviut — the soft undercoat of the muskox, spun into exceptionally warm, light yarn. Prized for modern knitwear.
- Layered design (inner layer fur-in, outer layer fur-out) traps body heat while allowing moisture to escape.

Contemporary Inuit clothing blends traditional designs with modern materials. Handmade parkas, sealskin mittens, and kamiik remain important cultural items and a significant source of income for artisans.

HOUSING
Historically, Inuit shelter varied by season and region:
- Iglu (snow-block house) — iconic dome of cut snow blocks, used primarily in winter by some groups. The word "igloo" comes from the Inuktitut "iglu," which simply means "house."
- Qarmaq — a semi-subterranean winter dwelling built of stone, sod, whalebone, or driftwood framing.
- Tupiq — a skin tent used in summer.

Today, Inuit live in modern houses in permanent communities, though hunting camps, cabins, and temporary shelters on the land remain important for harvesting trips. Housing shortages and overcrowding are significant issues in many northern communities.

TRANSPORT
- Qamutik — a traditional sled, historically pulled by sled dogs (qimmiq), with wooden or bone runners often coated with ice for smooth travel.
- Qajaq (kayak) — a one-person, skin-covered hunting vessel; the English word "kayak" comes directly from Inuktitut.
- Umiak — a larger, open skin boat used for whale hunting and moving families.
- Today, snowmobiles, ATVs, motorboats, and small planes are the primary means of transport. Qamutik sleds remain in use, now often towed by snowmobile.

Most Inuit communities are not connected by road to the rest of Canada; air travel and seasonal sealift (shipping) are the main links to the south.
