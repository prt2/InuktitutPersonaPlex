TOPIC: Notable Inuktitut-Speaking People
SOURCES: The Canadian Encyclopedia; Wikipedia; Office of the Governor General of Canada; Nunatsiaq News; Isuma TV

POLITICAL AND ADVOCACY LEADERS

Mary Simon (Ningiukudluk) — born 1947 in Kangiqsualujjuaq, Nunavik. 30th Governor General of Canada, installed July 26, 2021; the first Indigenous person to hold the role. She speaks English and Inuktitut as native languages. Before becoming Governor General, she was a CBC Northern Service broadcaster in the 1970s, a key negotiator of the James Bay and Northern Quebec Agreement, president of Makivik Corporation, Canada's first Ambassador for Circumpolar Affairs (1994–2003), Ambassador to Denmark (1999–2001), and president of Inuit Tapiriit Kanatami (2006–2012). Her Inuktitut name means "bossy little old lady," a self-deprecating joke she has shared publicly. She has championed the Inuktitut concept of ajuinnata — a promise never to give up.

Sheila Watt-Cloutier (Siila) — born 1953 in Kuujjuaq, Nunavik. Environmental and human-rights advocate; nominated for the Nobel Peace Prize in 2007. Former president of the Inuit Circumpolar Council (Canada) and chair of the ICC internationally, representing roughly 155,000 Inuit across Canada, Greenland, Alaska, and Chukotka. Led Inuit advocacy at the UN that contributed to the 2001 Stockholm Convention banning persistent organic pollutants. Author of "The Right to Be Cold" (2015). Raised traditionally for ten years before being sent south to residential schools. Co-author of the 1992 Silatunirmut: The Pathway to Wisdom report reforming Nunavik education.

Natan Obed — Inuk from Nain, Nunatsiavut. President of Inuit Tapiriit Kanatami since 2015, re-elected multiple times. Central figure in federal Inuit policy, including the Indigenous Languages Act, Inuit Nunangat Policy, and Inuit-Crown Partnership Committee.

Piita Irniq — elder, educator, and former Commissioner of Nunavut (2000–2005). A fluent Inuktitut speaker, former residential school survivor, and leading voice for cultural preservation.

Abe Okpik (Abraham Okpik), 1929–1997 — led Project Surname in 1968–1970, which restored family surnames to Inuit after the federal government's "disc number" (E-number) identification system was dismantled. Order of Canada recipient.

Lori Idlout — Member of Parliament for Nunavut, elected 2021 (NDP). Iqaluit-based lawyer and mental health advocate.

Aluki Kotierk — President of Nunavut Tunngavik Incorporated (NTI) since 2016. Leading voice for Inuktut language rights, education reform, and treaty implementation.

Jose Amaujaq Kusugak (1950–2011) — former president of Nunavut Tunngavik Incorporated; credited with popularizing the phrase "First Canadians, Canadians First." A key figure in Inuktitut broadcasting and language promotion.

ARTISTS

Kenojuak Ashevak (1927–2013) — Kinngait-based artist; one of Canada's most celebrated printmakers. Her 1960 print "The Enchanted Owl" became iconic. Companion of the Order of Canada. Featured on Canadian stamps and coins.

Pitseolak Ashoona (c. 1904–1983) — Kinngait artist known for drawings depicting traditional Inuit life. Published autobiography "Pictures Out of My Life" (1971).

Kananginak Pootoogook (1935–2010) — one of the first Inuit printmakers; began the co-operative movement in Kinngait and created iconic works documenting Inuit life.

Annie Pootoogook (1969–2016) — contemporary Kinngait artist whose drawings documented modern Arctic life. Won the Sobey Art Award in 2006.

Shuvinai Ashoona — born 1961 in Kinngait; contemporary artist whose vivid drawings blend Inuit tradition, science fiction, and contemporary life. 2022 Governor General's Award.

Ningiukulu Teevee — Kinngait storyteller-artist whose illustrations appear in children's books and gallery collections; daughter of a multigenerational Kinngait art family.

Ovilu Tunnillie (1949–2014) — rare female Inuit stone sculptor who gained national recognition; born in Kinngait area.

FILMMAKERS AND MEDIA

Zacharias Kunuk — born 1957 in Kapuivik, near Igloolik. Inuktitut filmmaker; co-founder of Igloolik Isuma Productions (1990), the first Inuit-owned production company in Canada. Directed Atanarjuat: The Fast Runner (2001), the first feature film written, produced, directed, and acted entirely in Inuktitut. Atanarjuat won the Caméra d'Or at Cannes 2001 and was named the greatest Canadian film of all time in a 2015 Toronto International Film Festival poll. Officer of the Order of Canada; Member of the Order of Nunavut. Other works include The Journals of Knud Rasmussen (2006), One Day in the Life of Noah Piugattuk (2019), and the animated Angakusajaujuq: The Shaman's Apprentice.

Alethea Arnaquq-Baril — Iqaluit-based filmmaker. Directed Angry Inuk (2016), about the seal hunt's role in Inuit life and its misrepresentation in Western media; won the Canadian Screen Award.

Paul Apak Angilirq (died 1998) — co-founder of Igloolik Isuma; screenwriter of Atanarjuat.

Pauloosie Qulitalik — Igloolik elder and co-founder of Isuma; featured in many Isuma films.

MUSICIANS

Tanya Tagaq — born 1975 in Cambridge Bay. Although Cambridge Bay is an Inuinnaqtun-speaking community (not primarily Inuktitut), Tagaq performs a distinctive solo form of Inuit throat singing and is widely associated with Inuit cultural revitalization. Won the Polaris Music Prize in 2014 for Animism. Author of Split Tooth (2018).

Susan Aglukark — born 1967; raised in Arviat, Nunavut (Kivalliq region, Inuktitut-speaking). Multiple Juno Award-winning singer-songwriter whose work blends Inuktitut and English. Her song "O Siem" became internationally known. Officer of the Order of Canada.

Elisapie Isaac — singer-songwriter from Salluit, Nunavik. Solo Inuktitut-English albums including "There Will Be Stars" (2018) and Inuktitut-language covers album "Inuktitut" (2023). Juno winner.

Beatrice Deer — Nunavik singer-songwriter (from Quaqtaq), blending traditional Inuktitut vocals with indie folk.

Lucie Idlout — Nunavut rock singer-songwriter.

The Jerry Cans — Iqaluit-based band mixing Inuktitut lyrics, throat singing, and folk/rock; prominent in contemporary Inuktitut music.

Qaggiavuut! — Nunavut performing arts society that trains and promotes Inuktitut-language performers.

LEGAL AND ACADEMIC

Murray Sinclair — while not Inuit, he is often mentioned alongside; but noting specifically Inuktitut-speaking: James Eetoolook (long-serving NTI vice-president), and Madeleine Redfern (former Iqaluit mayor, Arctic policy leader).

Madeleine Redfern — former mayor of Iqaluit (2010–2012, 2015–2019); active in Arctic policy, economic development, and digital infrastructure advocacy.

SCIENCE AND KNOWLEDGE

Enooyaq Sudlovenick — Nunavut Inuk marine biologist who applies Inuit Qaujimajatuqangit to research on Arctic marine mammals including beluga whales and ringed seals.

Leesee Papatsie — food security advocate from Iqaluit; founder of the Feeding My Family movement, a grassroots campaign drawing attention to the cost of food in the North.

Angela Hovak Johnston — leads the Inuit Tattoo Revitalization Project, reviving traditional kakiniit (women's tattoos) that had been suppressed under colonial and missionary pressure.
