TOPIC: Professions and Contributions
SOURCES: Inuit Tapiriit Kanatami; The Canadian Encyclopedia; Government of Nunavut; Nunavut Arts and Crafts Association

TRADITIONAL LIVELIHOODS
Before permanent settlement, the Inuit economy was based on hunting, fishing, and gathering. Skills were highly specialized:
- Hunters (angujjuktiit) — expert at tracking, reading sea ice, building hunting blinds, and navigating by landmarks, stars, wind patterns, and snowdrift shapes.
- Seamstresses — producing tailored waterproof and wind-resistant clothing from caribou, seal, and bird skins; essential to survival.
- Boat- and sled-builders — skilled in framing qajait (kayaks), umiaq (large skin boats), and qamutiik (sleds) from driftwood, bone, and skins.
- Toolmakers — carving harpoons, snow knives (pana), and cooking vessels from bone, antler, ivory, and stone.
- Midwives and healers (angakkuit) — caring for health, childbirth, and spiritual well-being.

CONTEMPORARY PROFESSIONS
Today Inuit work in the full range of modern professions. Key sectors include:

Government and public administration
- Nunavut's government is the largest employer in the territory; senior roles across Inuit regions are increasingly held by Inuit.
- Elected leadership: Members of the Legislative Assembly (Nunavut has a consensus-based, non-party legislature), regional corporation boards, municipal mayors, hamlet councils.

Health, education, social services
- Nurses, community health representatives, mental health workers, teachers, early childhood educators, and Inuktut language instructors. Inuit-led initiatives focus on training more Inuit into these roles.

Arts, crafts, and cultural industries
- Internationally recognized sculptors, printmakers, jewellers, filmmakers, musicians, writers. Cape Dorset (Kinngait) is a world centre of Inuit printmaking. Sealskin and fur-garment making supports many family-run businesses.

Resource industries and business
- Mining (Baffinland Iron Mines at Mary River, Agnico Eagle gold operations at Meadowbank and Meliadine, Raglan Mine in Nunavik) — a major employer in northern communities.
- Inuit-owned Birthright corporations (Makivik in Nunavik, Nunasi and the three Regional Inuit Associations in Nunavut, Inuvialuit Regional Corporation, Nunatsiavut Group of Companies) invest in airlines, fisheries, construction, shipping, hospitality, and financial services.
- Commercial fisheries — including turbot and shrimp in waters off Nunavut and Nunavik.

Media and communications
- Journalists and broadcasters at CBC North, APTN, Nunatsiaq News, Isuma TV. Radio remains a major community medium.

Science, research, and environmental stewardship
- Inuit Qaujimajatuqangit contributions to climate science, wildlife co-management, and polar research. Community-based monitoring programs are central to Arctic science.

Trades and skilled work
- Carpentry, heavy equipment operation, power plant operation, airport services, and aviation — all essential to the functioning of remote northern communities.

NOTABLE INUIT CONTRIBUTORS
- Mary Simon — Governor General of Canada (appointed 2021); long career in Inuit advocacy, president of ITK from 2006 to 2012.
- Sheila Watt-Cloutier — environmental and human-rights advocate; nominated for the Nobel Peace Prize in 2007 for work linking climate change and human rights.
- Natan Obed — president of Inuit Tapiriit Kanatami since 2015.
- Kenojuak Ashevak — one of Canada's most celebrated artists; her print "The Enchanted Owl" is iconic.
- Pitseolak Ashoona — artist known for graphic depictions of traditional Inuit life.
- Zacharias Kunuk — filmmaker; Atanarjuat: The Fast Runner won the Caméra d'Or at Cannes in 2001.
- Alethea Arnaquq-Baril — filmmaker (Angry Inuk, 2016) and producer.
- Tanya Tagaq — throat singer, Polaris Music Prize winner (2014).
- Susan Aglukark — multi-Juno-Award-winning singer.
- Abe Okpik — Order of Canada recipient; led Project Surname, restoring Inuit family names.
- Jose Kusugak — Inuit leader, credited with popularizing the phrase "First Canadians, Canadians First."
- Piita Irniq — former Commissioner of Nunavut; elder, educator, advocate.
- Lori Idlout — Member of Parliament for Nunavut (elected 2021).
- Leesee Papatsie — food security advocate, founder of the Feeding My Family movement.
